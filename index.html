About the client

The Polytechnic School at ASU.

The Polytechnic School (TPS) at Arizona State University is home to programs across engineering technology, computing, graphic information technology, and applied sciences. Experiential learning already happens across many of its programs, expressed through doing, reflecting, and applying.

But it happens inconsistently, under different names, with different levels of recognition, and without a unified story. The Experiential Learning Council has spent two semesters defining what ExL means at TPS. Now, they need your help bringing that vision to life.

Your work should be portfolio-ready and demonstrate professional execution across all deliverables. This is about translating design strategy into a compelling narrative that works in the real world.

What is experiential learning?

Learning by doing, and thinking about what you did.

Experiential learning is defined as learning from experience: learning by doing and then reflecting on it. Skills and knowledge are acquired through firsthand experiences rather than passive instruction. At TPS, this includes internships, capstone projects, co-ops, research, field practicums, service learning, and hands-on lab work across every program.

What makes TPS's approach distinct is the insistence on pairing practice with reflection (Principled Innovation™). Doing something is not enough. Students are expected to think critically about what they did, what they learned, and how it connects to theory and future practice. That cycle (doing, reflecting, applying) is the engine of ExL @ TPS.

The council's charge is to make this practice visible, recognized, and scalable across all TPS programs. Right now, it happens unevenly. Your team is being asked to design the brand, the digital experience, and the physical environment that will make ExL @ TPS impossible for faculty to miss and interested in seeking out.

Strategic Context

ExL @ TPS: SWOT analysis.

The council produced this analysis as part of their end-of-year recommendations. Use it as a strategic foundation for your positioning work. Every strength should be amplified, every gap should be addressed.

S
Strengths
  • TPS is a leader in hands-on learning
  • Faculty already engage in experiential work, but in fragmented ways
  • Existing industry connections and partnerships could be better utilized
  • Internship infrastructure could be strengthened and systematized

  • Inconsistent terminology and recognition

  • Career Services not integrated with ExL initiatives
O

  • New industrial park location (areas around TPS are rapidly developing)
  • Corporate sponsorships (partner with employers in the industrial park)

  • Grants and awards to faculty as recruitment and retention tools

  • Co-ops
T
Threats
  • Budget constraints for new staffing and operations
  • Faculty buy-in to systemic changes and loss of autonomy
  • Competition for student time and resources
  • ExL is currently perceived as "nice to have" versus essential

Council Recommendations

Where TPS is headed.

The Experiential Learning Council has identified clear short-term and long-term priorities for 2026/27. Your concept should be designed to support and accelerate these recommendations. Pick from at least three deliverables below your team would be best positioned to deliver.

Short-term priority

Hire a TPS Associate Director for ExL

No action needed.The initiative needs dedicated leadership to move from recommendation to reality. Design for a center that a director could step into and operate on day one.

Short-term priority

Develop ExL Branding & Communications

Develop branding and communication strategies for disseminating information to students and faculty about ExL. That's what you're building.

Short-term priority

Introduce ExL @ TPS at the TPS Advance

A faculty event at the start of Fall semester recognizing people doing ExL well. Design should account for recognition artifacts, awards, and presentation collateral.

Short-term

Develop ExL Workshops for Faculty

How could we get faculty to "experience" what experiential learning is like in 15-minutes at the TPS Advance? Is this a workshop, a demo, an exemplar? Craft some ideas for facilitation.

Short-term

Fund Collaborative Faculty Cohorts

Sponsor 5 grants to teams of 4–6 faculty, prioritizing cross-program collaboration. The brand should support this grant culture with templates and materials.

Short-term

Reconfigure the ExL Council

No action needed.Determine who to bring in, appoint a director of TPS ExL Initiatives. Your stakeholder section should reflect the current council membership, not the future.

Long-term vision

ExL @ TPS Center with Dedicated Director

A physical hub for everything related to ExL at TPS. Director and staff. Design the brand, ideas for future spaces, and a digital experience to support this vision from day one.

Long-term vision

ExL New Named Building

A new building focused on ExL with innovative learning spaces, indoor rotunda for community events. Create physical environment concepts that speak to this scale.

Long-term vision

Create More ExL Spaces Across Campus

Cross-collaborative spaces across TPS including atriums, classrooms, and workspaces. Make the Poly Library a more experiential space. Think ecosystem, not just building.

Scope of work

What your team is building.

All teams are working from the same brief, but only one concept will be selected as the direction TPS moves forward with. Select the deliverables (at least 3) your team is best positioned to complete. The final concept must feel unified across all deliverables.

  • 01
    Strategy and Research
    Competitive analysis of 5–7 peer institutions, brand positioning statement, mission/vision/values, brand pyramid, key messages for each audience, and a 3-year implementation roadmap aligned with the council's short-term and long-term recommendations.
    Foundation. Do not skip this or rush it.
  • 02
    Brand Identity
    Complete visual and verbal identity for the ExL initiative: naming recommendation, logo system, color system, typography, visual language, photography art direction, print templates, and a brand guidelines document detailed enough for someone outside your team to implement correctly.
    Every other deliverable is built on this. Inconsistency here shows everywhere.
  • 03
    Digital Experience
    Full UX/UI process for the ExL microsite extending ASU's existing digital presence. Site map, user flows, low-fidelity wireframes, high-fidelity prototype (mobile and desktop), usability testing with 10+ participants, and developer-ready handoff documentation.
    This is not a standalone site. It must extend asu.edu, not compete with it.
  • 04
    Physical Environment
    A concept for the future ExL Center on campus: space purpose and personality, interior branding, wayfinding system, zone programming (collaboration, focused work, presentations, community), and visualization through floor plan sketches, mood boards, or rendered concept imagery.
    It needs to be a vision. It does not need to be an architectural rendering.

Timeline

Lab 4.2

Revisions round 1

Honest reflection on what worked, what didn't, and what your team takes into the next project.

Metrics for success

How we'll judge it.

Six dimensions. Every team is evaluated on all six. The winning concept will be strong across all of them.

01

On-time delivery

All deliverables completed and presented by each milestone deadline. 100% of the work, on time, every time.

The council will review your work. Don't waste it.

02

Strategic depth

Every design decision traceable back to your research. The team can defend at least three strategic calls by citing specific findings.

Aesthetic preference is not a strategy. "Our audience of prospective students needs..." is.

03

Conceptual cohesion

A reviewer unfamiliar with the brief should be able to describe the brand consistently after looking at any single deliverable.

If the brand guidelines and the prototype look like two different projects, that's a problem.

04

Audience clarity

Every deliverable built with a specific audience in mind. The personas are not decorative; they are the reason decisions were made.

A faculty member and a prospective student want completely different things from this brand.

05

Quality of execution

Pixel-perfect, well-organized, and production-ready. Files that could be handed to a developer or printer without a follow-up email.

A developer opening your Figma file should feel relief, not confusion.

06

Creative risk

The concept does something peer institutions have not done. The team can identify one creative decision that pushed beyond the expected.

Safe work passes. Bold work gets selected. Design like the council is going to use this.

Notes from the stakeholders

Straight from the council.

  • Peer institutions are doing versions of this. Your job is to do it in a way that is distinctly TPS. Not a generic higher-ed campaign, not a polished PowerPoint, not a color swap on someone else's brand. Identify what makes TPS genuinely different and build from there. Differentiation is the work, not the decoration.

  • The competitive analysis, the personas, the journey map: none of these are boxes to check before you start designing. They are the reason your design decisions are credible. If you cannot connect a visual or structural choice back to a finding from your research, you are guessing. The council will ask you to defend your calls.

  • TPS ExL lives within a large, complex institutional system. Your microsite should feel like it belongs to asu.edu, sharing the same navigation patterns, accessibility standards, and underlying trust, while carrying its own identity and energy. The tension between institutional and distinctive is the design challenge. Solve it, do not avoid it.

  • Floor plans, mood boards, material samples, rendered concept images, wayfinding sketches: use whatever communicates the vision clearly. The question the council is asking is what it feels like to walk into this space. What makes someone want to work there instead of a library or a coffee shop? Make that feeling legible, not just described.

  • Every member is expected to contribute in the area where they are strongest. A student focused on UX should own the information architecture. A brand designer should be driving the visual identity. A researcher should be leading the competitive analysis. Be honest in your post-mortem about where you led and where you supported. The council evaluates teams, not individuals. A fractured team produces a fractured concept.

  • The winning concept will be reviewed for real-world application. That means your brand guidelines need to be detailed enough for someone outside your team to implement correctly. Your prototype needs to be functional enough for a developer to build from. Your print templates need to be production-ready. Design like the council is going to hand your files to someone on Monday morning.
